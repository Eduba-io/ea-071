The operating view

Most fintech products break down into three layers. Bank risk reviewers care which layer each decision lives on.

60

Traditional code

Payroll ingestion, advance calculations, ledger reconciliation, core integration plumbing. Postgres, queues, and careful engineering. Not AI.

30

Rule-based logic

Eligibility gates, advance caps, state-specific compliance rules, fee structures. Deterministic by design. A bank wants to read the rules.

10

Genuinely AI

The places where a model does something code and rules cannot. Name them, document them, and cut every other claim.

Your homepage calls Clockout an embedded EWA platform for financial institutions. Four core integrations (Q2, Jack Henry, Alkami, Candescent), with Fiserv in progress. A $2M seed closed last June led by Cofounders Capital. Nine people on the team. The LinkedIn page describes the product as AI-powered. The homepage does not say what the AI does. That gap is the thing worth a conversation.

Eduba's operating view across fintech engagements is a 60/30/10 split. Sixty percent of a typical product surface is traditional code and database work: payroll ingestion, advance calculations, ledger reconciliation, core integration plumbing. Thirty percent is rule-based logic: eligibility gates, advance caps, state-specific compliance rules, fee structures. Ten percent is genuinely AI: the places where a model can do something code and rules cannot. Bank risk reviewers care which layer each decision lives on. When a product says "AI-powered" without naming which layer, the reviewer assumes the worst and procurement slows. An afternoon to name the three or four places AI actually lives inside Clockout, describe them in language a bank risk committee will sign off on, and cut every other claim, protects the sales cycle.

Matt Creamer runs CRO at Eduba. Before any build, the engagement we would scope is a focused advisory sprint: two working sessions with Matt and Jake, one written artifact that turns the "AI-powered" line into a defensible layer-by-layer description, and a reusable bank risk-review pack that a solutions consultant (or a future hire) can walk through without pulling Clockout leadership into every call.

We did similar work with a product-company CTO earlier this year under a scoped sprint. The deliverables were a workshop, three advisory calls, an Organizational Context Architecture, and a Strategic Operations Framework. That engagement compressed a set of "we know we need to write this down" priorities into a week of written work the team used in procurement and in product.

The methodology we use is Interpretable Context Methodology. Submitted to ACM TiiS. Repo at github.com/RinDig/Interpretable-Context-Methodology-ICM-. It is how we think about agent context, audit trails, and the documentation that bank procurement actually reads. Eduba partners with NLP Logix for work that sits below the orchestration layer; NLP Logix has been in machine learning since 2011 and runs over 150 data scientists. If the right next step turns out to be payroll ingestion engineering rather than strategy, we say so and route the conversation.
